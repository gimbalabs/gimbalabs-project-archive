# Consent-Based Decision Making

Gimbalabs uses consent-based governance for collective decisions. This document explains how it works.

## What is Consent?

Consent means a proposal proceeds when **no one has a reasoned objection**. This is different from consensus (everyone agrees) or majority voting (most people agree).

A decision has consent when everyone can say: **"This is good enough for now, safe enough to try."**

## Why Consent?

- **Faster decisions** - We don't need everyone to love the idea, just no one to have genuine concerns
- **Better outcomes** - Objections surface real issues that improve proposals
- **More participation** - People engage because their concerns matter
- **Aligned with our values** - Experimentation over perfection

## The Consent Process

### 1. Present the Proposal

The proposer shares:
- What they want to do
- Why it matters
- How it will work
- What resources are needed

### 2. Clarifying Questions

Participants ask questions to understand the proposal. This is not the time for opinions or objections.

### 3. Reaction Round

Each participant shares their initial reaction:
- What do you like about this?
- What concerns do you have?
- What questions remain?

The proposer listens without responding (except brief clarifications).

### 4. Amend (Optional)

Based on reactions, the proposer may amend the proposal to address concerns.

### 5. Consent Round

The facilitator asks each participant: **"Do you have any objections to this proposal?"**

- **No objection** = "I can live with this" (not necessarily enthusiastic)
- **Objection** = "I have a genuine concern that needs to be addressed"

### 6. Integrate Objections

If objections are raised:
1. The objector explains their concern
2. The group explores how to address it
3. The proposal is modified
4. Return to consent round

If an objection cannot be integrated, the proposal doesn't proceed.

## Valid vs Invalid Objections

### Valid Objections

An objection is valid if it:
- Identifies a concrete harm or risk
- Shows how the proposal conflicts with our values/driver
- Points to missing information that's essential
- Raises a concern about implementation feasibility

**Examples:**
- "This timeline is unrealistic given our current commitments"
- "This contradicts our policy on X"
- "We don't have data on Y which is crucial to this decision"
- "This creates a conflict of interest that hasn't been addressed"

### Invalid Objections

These are **not** valid objections:
- Personal preferences ("I'd rather do it differently")
- Perfectionism ("There might be a better approach")
- Fear of change ("We've never done this before")
- Objecting on behalf of absent people
- Political or personal disagreements

**Invalid objections can be noted but don't block the proposal.**

## Timeframes

| Decision Type | Default Consent Period |
|--------------|------------------------|
| Internal proposals | 7 days |
| DRep votes | 3-7 days (see drep/process.md) |
| Governance changes | 14 days |
| Urgent decisions | 24-48 hours |

Consent periods can be shortened if:
- External deadlines require it
- The proposal has minimal impact
- Community agrees to expedite

## Where Consent Happens

- **Open Spaces** - Weekly Thursday meetings for synchronous consent
- **Discord** - Async consent for proposals with adequate discussion time
- **GitHub** - For process/documentation changes

## What Consent Applies To

We use consent for:
- Proposals that affect the community
- DRep voting decisions
- Resource allocation
- Process changes
- New projects or initiatives

We don't use consent for:
- Individual work decisions
- Technical implementation details (when direction is already approved)
- Emergency operational decisions

## Role of the Facilitator

The facilitator:
- Guides the group through each stage
- Ensures everyone can speak
- Determines if objections are valid
- Helps integrate objections into proposals
- Declares when consent is reached

## Documenting Consent

When a proposal passes:
1. Record the decision date
2. Note any objections and how they were addressed
3. Archive the final proposal with amendments
4. Track implementation in relevant project system

## When Consent Fails

If consent cannot be reached:
- The proposal may be withdrawn for revision
- It may be split into smaller decisions
- Additional information may be gathered
- A different approach may be explored

**No consent = no action** (we don't override or force through)
